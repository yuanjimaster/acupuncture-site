Acupuncture for Pain

The National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health provides information on acupuncture for pain treatment.

Since the 1970s when acupuncture arrived in the U.S., Western researchers have studied this ancient Chinese practice. The American public adopted it before science fully explained it, as conventional medicine had limitations. According to the 2002 National Interview Survey, approximately 8.2 million American adults used acupuncture, compared to 2.1 million the previous year.

Acupuncture treats diverse conditions: addiction, stroke, headache, menstrual cramps, tennis elbow, fibromyalgia, osteoarthritis, low back pain, carpal tunnel syndrome, asthma, infertility, pregnancy problems, dental pain, and cancer treatment side effects.

Peter Wayne, Ph.D., director of research at the New England School of Acupuncture, states: “The applications for acupuncture are endless…people use it” for sports injuries and emotional wellness.

Technology advancements have clarified how acupuncture adjusts tissues, nerves, and hormones. The NIH and World Health Organization formally approved certain acupuncture applications.

David S. Kiefer, MD, University of Arizona notes: “Sometimes they are surprised” at positive results, even skeptics report feeling good during treatments.

Back Pain

Back pain ranges from temporary to chronic. Acupuncture offers advantages by treating both pain symptoms and underlying causes, benefiting chronic sufferers.

Treatment plans vary based on pain severity, cause, and patient health, involving traditional acupuncture or combined acupuncture-exercise approaches aligned with natural healing philosophy. Many patients experience immediate results, increased mobility, and reduced pain within several treatments.

Standard treatments like ice, heat, anti-inflammatory medications, physical therapy, and exercises help, but acupuncture provides an alternative with proven effectiveness.

Evidence of Effective Acupuncture for Back Pain

A 2012 international study analyzed 29 studies with nearly 18,000 participants comparing acupuncture, sham acupuncture, and no treatment. Results showed acupuncture relieved pain by about 50%, published in Archives of Internal Medicine.

Dr. Lucy Chen, board-certified anesthesiologist and pain medicine specialist at Massachusetts General Hospital states: “I think the benefit of acupuncture is clear, and the complications and potential adverse effects of acupuncture are low compared with medication.”
